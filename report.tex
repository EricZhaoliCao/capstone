\begin{abstract}
In collaboration with Auros HK, we examine whether the funding-rate
mechanism of crypto perpetual swaps keeps the perpetual price anchored to
spot, and whether the resulting dislocations support tradeable
funding-rate arbitrage after realistic frictions.  We organise the
strategy space along two dimensions---asset class and exchange---and each
team member develops one strategy within it.  \textbf{Strategy~1} is a
rule-based, deferred-decision cross-exchange capture on
KAITO~(\textsc{hype}--\textsc{okex}) that earns an out-of-sample Sharpe of
$2.35$ but is fragile to slippage and timing.  \textbf{Strategy~2} blends a
funding-rate engine with a technical-momentum engine into a weekly,
dollar-neutral long/short CTREND book that returns $+48.2\%$ against a
$-66.3\%$ market (Sharpe $1.98$).  \textbf{Strategy~3} is a cost-aware
positive funding-basis carry on Binance (BTC/ETH/SOL) that, after real
spread calibration, reaches a normalised net PnL of $0.0462$ (Sharpe
$7.65$, max drawdown $-0.11\%$) by trading only the positive-funding,
positive-basis subset.  \textbf{Strategy~4} shows that the na\"ive
funding-rank cross-section \emph{loses} money on full PnL (Sharpe $-2.54$)
and builds two delta-neutral trades instead: a scalable cross-exchange
spread (Strategy~A, Sharpe $2.34$) and a negative cash-and-carry
(Strategy~B, in-regime $7.47$, honestly de-risked to $\approx 3$ and capped
at \$130{,}926).  Across all four, the common lesson is the same: funding
carry is real but small, survives only when price risk and execution cost
are accounted for honestly, and is bounded by capacity and regime rather
than by signal quality.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% =====================================================================
\partauthor{Liang Yingxi}{3035771024}

The expansion of derivative markets in digital assets has given rise to a
distinct class of instruments known as perpetual swaps.  Unlike
conventional futures contracts, perpetual swaps do not have an expiration
or settlement date.  To anchor their trading price to the underlying spot
index, exchanges rely on a funding-rate mechanism---a periodic payment
between long and short position holders, typically disbursed every four to
eight hours.  The magnitude and direction of the funding rate serve as a
corrective force, pushing the perpetual contract price back toward the
spot price without the need for delivery.

A direct implication of this mechanism is that funding rates for the same
underlying asset can diverge meaningfully across trading venues.  Such
divergence creates a potential arbitrage opportunity: by simultaneously
holding a long position on the exchange with the lower funding rate and a
short position on the exchange with the higher rate, one may capture the
rate differential while maintaining a market-neutral exposure.  Whether
this arbitrage remains economically viable after accounting for frictions
such as transaction costs, slippage, and borrowing fees constitutes an
empirical question of practical significance.

This capstone project was conducted in collaboration with Auros HK, a
leading market maker and trading firm specialising in cryptocurrency
markets.  Our mandate was to examine the effectiveness of the
funding-rate mechanism and to determine whether exploitable arbitrage
opportunities persist under real market conditions.  To address this, we
constructed a suite of trading strategies spanning both high-frequency and
low-frequency execution horizons.  The performance of these strategies was
rigorously evaluated using risk-adjusted metrics, notably the Sharpe
ratio.

Our set of trading strategies can be decomposed along two principal
dimensions: asset class and exchange.  Specifically, arbitrage
configurations may arise within the same asset on a single exchange,
across different exchanges for the same asset, or across distinct assets
on the same or different exchanges.  The core trading logic adheres to a
long--short structure---for instance, establishing a long position in the
perpetual with the cheaper funding rate while shorting the more expensive
one---and can be extended to combinations of perpetual swaps and spot
instruments.  Profit and loss for each strategy are calculated as the
funding income received less the associated costs, which primarily include
slippage, trading commissions, and borrowing expenses.
Table~\ref{tab:taxonomy} organises the configurations along the asset and
exchange dimensions, and Table~\ref{tab:longshort} summarises the
long--short structure of each trade.  The subsequent sections provide a
structured overview of each strategy in turn.

\section{Strategy 1: A Rule-Based Cross-Exchange Funding-Rate Capture}
\label{sec:strat1}
%% =====================================================================
\partauthor{Chen Junan}{3036555025}

As a low-risk, funding-harvesting complement to directional market-making
inventory at our partner Auros (a Binance VIP9 market maker), this strategy
exploits cross-exchange funding-rate differentials with a fully rule-based,
\emph{deferred-decision} design---deliberately avoiding predictive
modelling so that any profitability is attributable to the structure of the
funding mechanism itself.  Crypto perpetual markets exhibit larger and more
persistent funding dislocations than traditional venues
\cite{he2022perpetual, makarov2020trading}, and the recurring
funding-settlement cycle provides a time-predictable arbitrage window.

\subsection{Asset Selection}

We screen five liquid perpetual-swap tokens---AVAX, BERA, BTC, KAITO, and
PIPPIN---across all available cross-exchange combinations, yielding 43
candidate pairs.  For each pair we compute three metrics on the in-sample
period (March--October 2025): the average absolute funding spread, the
median round-trip bid--ask execution cost, and the net carry (spread minus
cost).

KAITO~\textsc{hype}--\textsc{okex} stands out: it exhibits the largest
average funding spread ($4.75$~bps) while execution cost remains contained
($3.98$~bps), producing the only positive net carry ($+0.76$~bps) among
high-spread candidates.  By contrast, BTC pairs offer positive net carry
but spreads are too small ($\approx 0.5$~bps) to survive realistic
slippage, whereas altcoin pairs such as BERA and PIPPIN have wide spreads
but costs that exceed them by a factor of two to ten because of
illiquidity.  Consequently, we select KAITO~\textsc{hype}--\textsc{okex}
as the trading universe for the remainder of this strategy.

\subsection{Strategy Design}

The strategy follows a \emph{deferred-decision} framework.  Rather than
acting immediately when a funding rate is published (e.g.\ 08:00~UTC), the
model waits until shortly before the next settlement, allowing the order
book to reveal its true state before capital is committed.

\subsubsection{Timing and execution flow}

An observation window opens at the funding-event time (e.g.\ 08:00).
Minute-level basis observations (mid-price differences between the two
exchanges) are accumulated continuously.  At three minutes before
settlement the strategy evaluates its entry filters; if they pass, it
executes on the next market tick and holds the position through settlement
to capture the funding differential.

\subsubsection{Entry filters}

A trade is initiated only if all four conditions are satisfied:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{Spread exceeds cost.}  The absolute funding spread must
        clear the round-trip transaction cost, otherwise the expected
        value after fees is negative.\footnote{Round-trip cost
        $= 2\times$ (\textsc{hype} taker fee $2.5$~bps $+$ \textsc{okex}
        taker fee $1.5$~bps) $= 8$~bps.}
  \item \emph{Minimum observations.}  At least ten minutes of basis data
        must be available in the window so that volatility and trend
        estimates are statistically reliable.
  \item \emph{Basis-volatility filter.}  If the standard deviation of
        intraday basis observations exceeds $20$~bps, the opportunity is
        skipped; high noise renders the spread estimate unreliable.
  \item \emph{Trend and drift guard.}  If basis is drifting adversely
        against the intended trade direction---more than $5$~bps in trend
        or $10$~bps in cumulative adverse drift---the signal is discarded.
        Capturing a funding spread is economically meaningless if
        cross-exchange price risk erodes the profit before settlement.
\end{enumerate}

In the best KAITO configuration the basis-volatility, trend, and drift
filters are set to permissive values, effectively disabling them; the
robustness analysis in Section~\ref{sec:s1-robust} shows why tighter
filters \emph{reduce} performance on this pair.

\subsubsection{Exit rules}

The core exit mechanism is z-score mean reversion: the position is closed
when the funding-spread z-score reverts to zero, using a 30-period rolling
mean and standard deviation.  A maximum holding cap of 100 funding periods
(approximately 33 days) forces exit to preserve capital efficiency.  An
optional cost-based exit closes the position early if the absolute spread
falls back below round-trip cost.

\subsubsection{Position sizing}

Notional is scaled dynamically by the expected-return-to-volatility ratio:
\begin{equation}
  N = N_{\text{base}}\cdot
      \text{clamp}\!\left(\frac{\mathrm{ER}}{\text{target}_{\mathrm{ER}}},
      \,0.25,\,4.0\right),
  \label{eq:s1-sizing}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{ER} = (\text{spread} - \text{total cost})/\text{basis
volatility}$ and $\text{target}_{\mathrm{ER}} = 0.5$~bps.  The base
notional is \$100k per leg.  This concentrates capital in high-conviction
opportunities and scales down when the signal is weak.

\subsubsection{Risk management}

A risk manager enforces three hard constraints: a basis stop at $50$~bps
(the position is liquidated if the cross-exchange price basis moves
adversely by more than $50$~bps), a maximum holding period of $720$~hours,
and take-profit / stop-loss thresholds at $2\%$ / $1\%$.  In practice the
basis stop and the z-score exit are the most frequently triggered close
reasons.

All 19 out-of-sample trades were initiated in the same direction---long

rule only enters when the sign of the spread is stable through the decision
window.

\subsection{Sensitivity Analysis}

We conduct two stress tests to assess how fragile the strategy's edge is
to execution assumptions.

\subsubsection{Slippage sensitivity}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/cja_fee_sensitivity.png}
  \caption{\textbf{Sensitivity to extra slippage per fill.}  Green solid
           line: strategy blind to slippage; blue dashed line: strategy
           adapts its entry threshold.}
  \label{fig:s1-fee}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:s1-fee} compares two behaviours under varying extra
slippage per fill (from $-4$~bps to $+15$~bps).  The \emph{blind} strategy
(green) uses a fixed cost threshold and ignores additional slippage; its
Sharpe collapses from above $4$ to below $-4$ as execution quality
deteriorates.  The \emph{adaptive} strategy (blue) internalises the extra
cost into its entry filter; its Sharpe stays near zero across the full
range, but profit opportunity is compressed to virtually nothing.  The
takeaway is sharp: above approximately $+5$~bps of extra slippage, the
blind strategy is no longer viable, and the adaptive version preserves
capital but offers no meaningful return.

\subsubsection{Timing sensitivity}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/cja_timing_sensitivity.png}
  \caption{\textbf{Sharpe versus decision timing.}  The vertical dashed

  \label{fig:s1-timing}

Figure~\ref{fig:s1-timing} plots Sharpe against the decision point's

decisions placed too close to settlement leave insufficient time for
execution.  Both dimensions are binding: the strategy requires execution
costs near base fee \emph{and} a decision exactly three minutes before
settlement.

\subsection{Limitations}

The results above should be interpreted with four caveats.  First, the
backtest assumes instantaneous full taker execution; in practice a \$100k
single-shot order on KAITO may walk the book, producing fills
significantly worse than the bid--ask mid (mitigations include order
slicing or earlier entry, though the latter reintroduces price risk).
Second, the optimal decision window (3 minutes) and the finding that
filters are counterproductive were validated only on KAITO over a
four-month test period, so cross-asset and longer-horizon robustness are
untested.  Third, we lack spot/index price data, which prevents exact
replication of the exchange funding calculation and biases the running
proxy.  Fourth, the strategy is intentionally simple---fully rule-based,
without predictive modelling---and serves as a transparent baseline against
which more sophisticated approaches can be benchmarked.

\section{Strategy 2: A Dual-Engine Funding-plus-Momentum Long/Short Strategy}
\label{sec:strat2}
%% =====================================================================
\begingroup\par\vspace{-2pt}%
{\small\itshape Deng Xuying\ (Student ID: 3036555556)\quad
 Liang Jiarui\ (Student ID: 3036549789)}\par\vspace{4pt}\endgroup

This strategy asks whether funding-rate information can be systematically
combined with technical trend signals.  Building on the CTREND framework,
we develop a dual-engine architecture: a Funding-Rate (FR) engine that
captures same-asset cross-venue funding dislocations across major
exchanges, and a Technical-Momentum (TE) engine that serves as a
regularised trend anchor.  The two are blended into a single cross-sectional
CTREND score and traded as a weekly-rebalanced, dollar-neutral long/short
book with bid--ask-adjusted execution and a data-driven fee model.  Over 53
weeks (February 2025--March 2026) the strategy returns a cumulative
$+48.2\%$ against an equal-weight market that fell $-66.3\%$, with an
annualised Sharpe of $1.98$ and a maximum drawdown of $11.1\%$.

\subsection{Model Architecture}

The design assumes that trend persistence and funding dynamics capture
different information.  The \emph{TE engine} follows CTREND with nine
technical indicators (RSI, CCI, MACD-related measures, moving-average
ratios, and Bollinger Band Width); each is converted into a
cross-sectional forecast through a six-week Fama--MacBeth procedure, after
which ElasticNet regularisation ($L_1$ ratio $=0.3$) selects the most
informative predictors.  The \emph{FR engine} uses four inherently
cross-sectional funding features---same-asset cross-venue funding rank
(\texttt{fr\_sa\_cv}), funding spread (\texttt{fr\_xspread}), funding
deviation (\texttt{fr\_xdev}), and funding z-score (\texttt{fr\_xz})---
aggregated with an expanding-window information-coefficient weighting
scheme.  Both signals are cross-sectionally standardised and combined as
\begin{equation}
  \text{CTREND} = 0.5\,Z_{\mathrm{TE}} + 0.5\,Z_{\mathrm{FR}},
  \label{eq:ctrend}
\end{equation}
with the equal weighting as the baseline.  The composite is then smoothed
with an eight-week rolling average to stabilise the cross-sectional
rankings.

\subsection{Data and Universe}

The analysis uses the Auros resampled daily BBO dataset (with funding rates
embedded) spanning February 2025 to March 2026, across six major exchanges
and focusing on perpetual contracts with enough cross-exchange coverage to
support same-asset cross-venue funding analysis
(Table~\ref{tab:fm-data}).

\subsection{Portfolio Construction and Risk Controls}

Each week, assets are ranked by their smoothed CTREND scores and a
dollar-neutral book is formed by longing the top quintile (Q5) and
shorting the bottom quintile (Q1), equally weighted within each leg.  A
funding overlay then manages position sizes dynamically: long positions
are scaled up as funding rises above predefined thresholds and short
positions as funding falls increasingly negative.  A hard panic-exit
forcibly liquidates positions when funding breaches extreme stress levels
($\pm 0.1500\%$/day), providing protection against sudden funding-regime
shifts.

reversals.  Finally, a true same-asset cross-venue book is operationally
intensive: moving capital across six exchanges introduces latency and
margin-fragmentation costs that a backtest cannot fully model.

\section{Strategy 3: A Cost-Aware Positive Funding-Basis Carry}
\label{sec:strat3}
%% =====================================================================
\partauthor{Liang Mengxuan}{3036551495}

This strategy asks a deliberately demanding question: after realistic
transaction costs, can a funding-basis dislocation on Binance support a
tradeable rule?  The empirical setting is a funding-interval panel for
BTCUSDT, ETHUSDT, and SOLUSDT over calendar year 2024, with each funding
observation aligned to the spot and perpetual close of the \emph{previous}
hour to avoid look-ahead.  A separate Binance futures order-book snapshot
(30~Jan--1~Mar 2026) is used purely to calibrate a realistic symbol-level
spread, and is treated as a cost proxy rather than as exact 2024 execution
evidence.

\subsection{Data and Variable Construction}

The main panel uses 3{,}294 funding observations and 26{,}352 hourly
perpetual and spot klines across the three symbols
(Table~\ref{tab:s2-data}).  The central state variable is the basis,
$\text{basis}_t = \text{perp\_close}_t / \text{spot\_close}_{t-1} - 1$: a
positive basis means the perpetual trades above spot.  For a long-spot /
short-perpetual position, the standardised next-period gross PnL is
$\text{funding}_{t+1} + \text{spot\_ret}_{t+1} - \text{perp\_ret}_{t+1}$,
from which a turnover cost is deducted whenever the position changes.

\subsection{Strategy Design}

We evaluate strategy design as an economic question, not merely a
performance ranking.  A simple full-direction funding rule trades both
positive and negative funding, but it implicitly assumes that the
short-spot (negative-funding) side is as executable as the long-spot side,
which is questionable once spot-borrowing costs are absent from the data.
The final rule therefore trades only the \emph{positive} funding-basis
direction: it enters a long-spot / short-perpetual position only when
funding is positive and above its causal rolling threshold \emph{and} the
basis is positive and above its causal rolling threshold
(Table~\ref{tab:s2-strats}).  In words: enter only when the trader is paid
to be short the perpetual \emph{and} the perpetual is already expensive
relative to spot.

pair---a $0.20$ funding quantile and a $0.30$ basis quantile---is chosen on

simpler positive-funding benchmark, but the trade is narrower, less
active, and easier to interpret as a feasible positive funding-basis
carry.

\subsection{Backtesting Framework and Cost Calibration}

The backtest uses the funding interval as the event clock.  At each
timestamp and symbol, the strategy observes the current funding and basis
(computed from the previous-hour close) and decides whether to hold a
position over the next interval; portfolio return is the equal-weighted
average of symbol-level net PnL.  Returns are annualised over
$365\times 3$ funding intervals, and the sample is split on 1~Oct~2024 for
out-of-sample evaluation.

thin.  Each leg is charged $1.5$~bps (so $3.0$~bps round-trip), plus a
symbol-specific futures half-spread estimated from the 2026 order-book

positive funding-basis carry emerges as a meaningful research-grade
strategy.  Its credibility, however, depends on those disciplines---and on
its deliberately conservative scope, which avoids the negative-funding side
that would require unmodelled spot borrowing.

\section{Strategy 4: Cross-Exchange Spread Capture and Negative Cash-and-Carry}
\label{sec:strat4}
%% =====================================================================
\partauthor{Cao Zhaoli}{3036668420}

This part takes the cross-exchange and cash-and-carry cells of the
taxonomy in Table~\ref{tab:taxonomy} to their conclusion.  I build a
cross-exchange funding-rate arbitrage on 20 perpetual coins and six venues
over one year.  The na\"ive idea---rank coins by funding and trade the
extremes---looks like free money but loses money (full-PnL Sharpe $-2.54$)
once every leg's price risk is counted; many refinements, including a
machine-learning model, all fail out of sample.  Two delta-neutral
strategies survive: \textbf{Strategy~A} (same coin, two venues) reaches an
out-of-sample Sharpe of $2.34$ at any size, while \textbf{Strategy~B}
(negative cash-and-carry) reaches $7.47$ in regime but is dormant $78\%$
of the time and capped at \$130{,}926, which an honest risk pipeline cuts
to a single-year range of $[2.41, 3.81]$.  The remainder of this section
develops the data and engine, the na\"ive baseline, the failed attempts,
both working strategies, and a full risk and robustness analysis.

\subsection{Background and Related Literature}
\label{sec:background}
%% =====================================================================

\subsubsection{The funding mechanism}
\label{sec:mechanism}

Let $P_t$ be the mark price and $I_t$ the spot index.  In each interval the exchange
sets a funding rate $f_t$, which is a pemium term plus an interest term,
\begin{equation}
  f_t \;\approx\; \text{clip}\!\Big(\tfrac{P_t - I_t}{I_t} + r^{\text{int}}_t,
       \; -\bar f,\; +\bar f\Big),
  \label{eq:funding_def}
\end{equation}
where $\bar f$ is the cap.  A long position of size $N$ gets the cash flow
\begin{equation}
  \pi^{\text{long}}_t = -f_t\,N, \qquad
  \pi^{\text{short}}_t = +f_t\,N ,
  \label{eq:funding}
\end{equation}
so a long collects funding when $f_t < 0$ and a short collects when $f_t > 0$.
\cite{ackerer2024perpetual} show that funding equals the cost of carry of a synthetic
spot-plus-financing position.  The key point is this: Equation~\eqref{eq:funding} is
\emph{only} the funding leg.  The total PnL of the perp also has a price term and a
cost term,
\begin{equation}
  \pi^{\text{total}}_t = \underbrace{-f_t N}_{\text{funding}}
                       + \underbrace{N\,\tfrac{P_{t+1}-P_t}{P_t}}_{\text{price}}
                       - \underbrace{c_t}_{\text{cost}} .
  \label{eq:total}
\end{equation}
So if a strategy reports only the funding term, the, number is not real, unless the
price term is already removed in another way.

\subsubsection{Perpetual pricing and the basis}

Define the basis $b_t = (P_t - I_t)/I_t$.  Two facts are useful.  The same-coin
same-venue basis (a perp against its own spot) is small and mean-reverts, so a
single-venue hedge is clean---this is the base of Strategy~B.  The same-coin
cross-venue basiis (one exchange's perp against another's) is larger and slower,
because to close it I need capital on two venus at the same time
\cite{makarov2020trading}---this is the base of Strategy~A.

\subsubsection{Carry, dispersion and the cross-section}

Three lines of literature shape the design.  The \emph{carry} line
\cite{schmeling2023crypto} treats funding as a risk premium that depends on the state.
The \emph{arbitrage and dispersion} line \cite{makarov2020trading} shows that
cross-exchange gaps mean-revert but are limited by how fast capital can move.  The
\emph{factor} line \cite{liu2022common, borri2022cross} prices the cross-section, and
I use it as a benchmark.  I also use \cite{moreira2017volatility} for sizing
\cite{almgren2001optimal} for cost, \cite{foucault2017toxic} and
\cite{bailey2014deflated} for honest testing, \cite{sebastiao2021forecasting} for the
ML part, and \cite{hu2023crypto} for crowding.

\subsubsection{Funding as a price of leverage}

Funding is the interest of leverage: a long perp is a spot long with leverage, and
without frictions funding would be near the risk-free rate \cite{ackerer2024perpetual}.
The large and often \emph{negative} funding I find (Section~\ref{sec:eda}) is the gap
from this benchmark, and its size shows how strong the frictions are.  Negative
funding means traders are willing to pay to be short, that is, the natural longs are
too few.  So to harvest funding is to give missing this leverage---it is a paid risk,
not a free profit.

\subsubsection{How this paper relates to prior work}

Most crypto-arbitrage papers study the \emph{price} gap across venues
\cite{makarov2020trading}.  Strategy~A moves this idea to the \emph{funding} basis: I
trade the slow and lasting cross-venue funding difference, not thhe fast price gap.  So
the contribution of this paper is not a new anomaly, but a careful, friction-aware
build and de-risk of two usable strategies, with an honest count of how much edge is
left after price risk, borrow limit, and regime.

\subsubsection{The 4-hour decision grid}

Funding setltes on different clocks (from hourly to 8-hourly), so I use a 4-hour grid
that lets every venue fall on a bin edge.  To avoid look-ahead, the funding I collect
in a bin is set just before the, bin ends so the rate I see when I decide is never the
rate that pays.  The out-of-sample (OOS) period is the lsat six months (1{,}086 bins);
the first six months are in-sample, for any parameter I must choose.

\subsubsection{The backtest engine}

All strategies usse one engine.  At each bin it computes a signal chooses positions,
and holds them with a \emph{stickiness} rule: a position opens only above an entry
threshold $\theta_{\text{ent}}$, and closes only below an eit threshold
$\theta_{\text{ext}} < \theta_{\text{ent}}$, which is the toxic-arbitrage buffer of
\cite{foucault2017toxic}.  The sizing follows \cite{moreira2017volatility},
\begin{equation}
  w_{i,t} \;=\; \text{clip}\!\Big(\frac{v^\star}{\hat\sigma_{i,t}},
              \; w_{\min},\; w_{\max}\Big),
  \label{eq:voltarget}
\end{equation}
with $v^\star = 150$~bp, $w_{\min}=0.3$, $w_{\max}=1.5$, and $\hat\sigma_{i,t}$ lagged
by one bin.

\subsubsection{Cost and fill model}

Every leg pays the VIP9 maker fee plus 1~bp slippage, and I count the, round-trip cost
only when a possition changes,
\begin{equation}
  c_t \;=\; \sum_{i \in \Delta_t}\big(\text{maker}_{e(i)} + 1\,\text{bp}\big),
  \label{eq:cost}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta_t$ is the legs opened or closed at $t$.  Fills are a probability, not a
sure thing: a signalled entry is taken with probability $0.8$, and the entry-bin price
PnL is cut by 12.5\%.  All Sharpe ratios are after cost and are annualised with
$\sqrt{365\cdot 24/4}$.

\subsubsection{Evaluation philosophy}

The main number is the annualised Sharpe,
\begin{equation}
  \text{Sh} \;=\; \frac{\bar r}{\hat\sigma_r}\,\sqrt{N_{\text{yr}}},
  \qquad N_{\text{yr}} = \frac{365\cdot 24}{4}.
  \label{eq:sharpe}
\end{equation}
Two choices come again and again.  For an on-off strategy (B) I report a bootstrapped
\emph{range}, not one point, because the in-regime Sharpe is not what a live book
earns.  For any regression I use Newey--West errors \cite{newey1987simple}, because
funding and basis are autocorrrelated.

\subsubsection{Look-ahead controls and validation}

Overfitting can come from three doors, and I close each one.  \emph{Future
information}: every signal uses only data before $t$ (the collected funding, the
lagged volatility, and the forward price).  \emph{Parameter selection}: I choose
parameters on the first six months only, and report all results on the untouched last
six months.  \emph{Survivorship}: Section~\ref{sec:risk} adds phantom delistings at a
live-measured rate.  Because I tried many variants, I treat the winners as upper
bounds and report the deflated Sharpe \cite{bailey2014deflated}.

\subsection{Strategy A: Cross-Exchange Funding-Spread Capture}
\label{sec:stratA}
%% =====================================================================

\subsubsection{Construction}

Strategy~A keeps the funding income but removes the price, by trading the \emph{same
coin} on two venues  For each coin I watch the cross-exchange spread
$g = \max_e f_{e} - \min_e f_{e}$.  When $g \ge 3$~bp I opn a delta-neutral pair: I
long the perp on the lowest-funding venue (it pays me as a long) and short the perp on
the highest-funding venue (it pays me as a short).  Because both legs are the same
coin, the price cancels, and what is left is the funding difference plus a small
cross-exchange basis that mean-reverts \cite{makarov2020trading}.  I hold unitl the
spread turns over or a $1.5$~bp exit \cite{foucault2017toxic}, size by
Equation~\eqref{eq:voltarget}, and use only the eight most liquid coins.

\subsubsection{Performance}

In the six-month OOS window, Strategy~A gets a Sharpe of $\mathbf{2.34}$, $8.8\%$ a
year, $3.7\%$ volatility, and a max drawdown of only $-0.9\%$ (Table~\ref{tab:headline},
Figure~\ref{fig:Aequity})---a stable relative-value profile, the opposite of the
baseline.

\begin{table}[htbp]
  \centering

\subsubsection{Construction}

Strategy~B removes the price inside a \emph{single} venue.  On OKX, when funding is
deeply negative ($f \le -8$~bp), I long the perp (collecting $-f$) and short the spot
of the saame coin, and I fund the, short by borrowing the coin.  The delta cancels, and
what is lfet is the funding miuns the borrow cost; I exit when funding rises above
$-2.4$~bp.  Because the hedge is against the coin's own spot, the left basis is much
smaller than A's cross-venue one, so B is almost a pure funding play.

The borrow leg is both the cost and teh limit.  Borrow rates change over time (so I
use the real path), they rise when funding is most negative (I stress-test up to
$5\times$), and they have a quota (the pool can run out).  The first two are
survivable; teh thid one is the real limit (Section~\ref{sec:risk}).

\subsection{Economic Interpretation}
\label{sec:econ}
%% =====================================================================

Why does this edge exist, and why is it not taken away by other people?  In my
understanding, two the strategies are not easy money.  I can earn thhe return because
I tke a risk that other people do not want to take.  Two ideas can explain it: carry
is a kind of risk premium \cite{koijen2018carry}, and arbitrage has its limits
\cite{shleifer1997limits}.

\subsubsection{Where the return comes from}

In Strategy~A, I am like a person standing between two venues.  On one venue there
are too many longs and on the other venue there are too many shorts, so both sides
pay funding to me.  In Strategy~B, the retail longs on the meme cins are too
crowded, so they pay a high funding to keep their position, and I take the other
side.  So the return is not from prediction.  It is from giving the side that is
missing.  This is also teh reason why the machine-learning model did not help.

\subsubsection{The risks I take}

As \cite{koijen2018carry} say, carry can earn a premium because it loses money in
bad times.  My strategies also take some risks like tis.  When a coin drops very
fast, the hedge may break (Table~\ref{tab:blowup}).  The borrow cosst may rise very
high, or the pool may be used up, just when the trade wants to grow
\cite{brunnermeier2009market, frazzini2014betting}.  The carry is also closed for
most of the time \cite{schmeling2023crypto}.  And if fails OKX one time, the whole B
book can be lost.  The return is the price I get for taking these risks.

\subsubsection{Why the cross-exchange spread persists}

To close A's spread, I need to put margin on two, venues at the same tmie, and this
money cannot be shared.  So the gap closes very slowly and stays for a long tiime
\cite{makarov2020trading}; this is the crypto version of capital that moves slowly
\cite{duffie2010presidential}.  In simple words, the spread is the money I get for
putting capital on two venues at the same time, and this is also why the trrade
cannot be too large---it is limited by depth.

\subsubsection{Why deeply-negative funding persists}

To get B's carry, I must short the spot, and to short the spot I must borrow the
coin.  But for these small coins, the amount I can borow is very small, only about
\$130k (Table~\ref{tab:capacity}), and the rate is high.  So no one can do this trade
in a large size, and the negative, funding do not go awy.  The borrow limit is
exactly the limit to arbitrage \cite{frazzini2014betting}.

\subsubsection{Benchmarking against the literature}

Other crypto papers report a Sharpe of about $1$--$2$
\cite{liu2022common, schmeling2023crypto} (Table~\ref{tab:bench}).  My Strategy~A
($2.34$) and Strategy~B ($\approx 3$) are higher, but the main reason is that they
are narrower and have smaller capacity.

\subsection{Implementation Considerations}
\label{sec:impl}
%% =====================================================================

A backtest is only a hypothesis, not the real system.  Below I write what a real
deployment needs.

\subsubsection{Execution}

Both strategies, make a decision every 4 hours, but inside teh bin the order should be
done slowly, maker first.  This is the most important for Strategy~A, because it only
breks even near $2.7$~bp; if I pay the taker spread, the Sharpe falls from about
$2.3$ to about $1$.  Strategy~B is easier here, but it has one more problem: I must
borrow the coin for the short leg.

\subsubsection{Monitoring}

Every risk should have a live monitor: close the position automatically when a coin
drops fast, a cap for each venue, a check on the borrow rate, a tracker for the
regime, and a daily stop-loss.

\subsubsection{Operational and custody risk}

The biggest loss is not from the market but from operation: if one exchange, fails,
the whole B book can be lost.  Strategy~B earns only about \$50k per year, but it can
lose \$130k in one event.  So the main job is to spread the money across exchanges
and counterparties, not to improve the signal.

\subsubsection{A staged go-live plan}

I would not use the full size directly, from the backtest.  The plan have several
steps: first a paper test to check the real fills, then a small-money test below the
B cap, and only after that scale up Strategy~A (Strategy~B cannot scale).  Every step
has a fixed target to pass.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limits}
%% =====================================================================

I list my main worries, the most serious one first.

\emph{Execution.}  Strategy~A's $2.34$ uses the average price inside the bin; if I
use only one single price, it falls to about $1.4$.  My fill assummption ($0.8$) is
also too simple.

\emph{Only one regime and one year.}  The out-of-sample period is only six months,
and crypto carry depends a lot on teh regime, so another year may be very different.

\emph{Too many trials.}  Because I tried many variants, after the correction
Strategy~A's Sharpe falls to $0.68$ (below $0.95$).  So the $2.34$ is only a hint,
not a sure result, the and way to fix it is more data not more models.

\emph{Data.}  The 4-hour grid is a little rough, and all the funding data come from
only one source.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% =====================================================================

Taken together, the four strategies map the funding-rate arbitrage space
of Table~\ref{tab:taxonomy} and reach a consistent verdict.  The funding
mechanism does anchor perpetual prices to spot, but only weakly: the
direct convergence pull is small (Strategy~3 finds a same-sign basis
convergence edge of barely one percentage point), and the carry it pays is
modest once price risk and execution cost are charged honestly.  Naive
harvesting fails---Strategy~4 shows a funding-rank cross-section losing
$-2.54$ on full PnL---so every viable trade in this report is built around
removing price risk rather than predicting it.

Each member exploits a different cell of the taxonomy.
\emph{Strategy~1} (Chen Junan) captures the cross-exchange funding
\emph{spread} on a single carefully screened pair,
KAITO~\textsc{hype}--\textsc{okex}, with a deferred-decision rule timed
three minutes before settlement; it earns an out-of-sample Sharpe of
$2.35$ but its edge is thin and dies above $\sim 5$~bps of extra slippage.
\emph{Strategy~2} (Deng Xuying and Liang Jiarui) blends a funding-rate
engine with a technical-momentum engine into a weekly, dollar-neutral
CTREND long/short book; over a falling market ($-66.3\%$) it returns
$+48.2\%$ at a Sharpe of $1.98$, with the funding overlay smoothing returns
when the trend engine struggles.  \emph{Strategy~3} (Liang Mengxuan) trades
a positive funding-\emph{basis} carry on Binance majors, deliberately
restricted to the long-spot / short-perpetual side so that no unmodelled
spot borrowing is required; with real spread calibration it delivers a
stable normalised net PnL of $0.0462$ (Sharpe $7.65$, drawdown $-0.11\%$)
and survives train--test and walk-forward checks.  \emph{Strategy~4} (Cao
Zhaoli) trades the same-coin cross-venue funding spread at scale
(Strategy~A, Sharpe $2.34$, any size) and a single-venue negative
cash-and-carry (Strategy~B), whose headline in-regime $7.47$ is honestly
deflated by capacity and regime to a long-run $\approx 3$ within
$[2.41, 3.81]$ and a hard \$130{,}926 cap.

Three lessons recur across all of our work.  First, accounting honesty
dominates signal cleverness: a funding-only PnL can overstate the Sharpe by
more than twenty points, and a fee-only backtest can manufacture a profit
that real spreads erase.  Second, the durable edge lives in delta-neutral
\emph{structure}---cross-exchange spreads, cash-and-carry against own
spot---not in forecasting, which is why machine-learning overlays added
nothing.  Third, what bounds these strategies is not the quality of the
signal but capacity, borrow limits, and regime: the same frictions that
keep the funding anomaly alive are what stop anyone from arbitraging it
away.  Funding-rate arbitrage is therefore real but conditional, and is
best understood---consistent with Auros's mandate---as a set of
capacity-aware, risk-calibrated relative-value trades rather than as free
carry.
